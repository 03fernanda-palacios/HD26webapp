Agricultural Productivity Losses from Wildfire Smoke: Evidence from the 2018 Camp Fire

Chen, L.  ·  Martinez, R.  ·  Singh, A. K.

UC Davis, Department of Agricultural & Resource Economics; NBER Working Paper No. 26901

DOI: 10.3386/w26901  |  Submitted: March 2019  |  Status: Under Review

Abstract

We estimate agricultural productivity losses attributable to fine particulate matter (PM2.5) exposure during the November 2018 Camp Fire — the most destructive wildfire event in California history. Using a difference-in-differences framework applied to USDA-NASS survey data for Butte, Glenn, Colusa, and Tehama counties (n = 2,841 farms), we find that almond yields declined 22.4% (p < 0.001) and walnut yields declined 17.8% (p < 0.01) in the 2018 harvest season relative to the synthetic control. Rice acreage harvested fell 18.2%, consistent with early field abandonment due to equipment failure and labor displacement. Total estimated losses amount to $184.6 million (2018 USD) in direct crop value, with multiplier effects yielding a regional economic loss of $312.1 million. We document a strong dose-response relationship between cumulative AOD exposure (NASA MODIS Terra) and yield loss across commodities, with elasticity estimates ranging from −0.31 to −0.58. These findings have direct implications for federal crop insurance actuarial risk models and USDA emergency loan eligibility criteria.

1. Introduction

The Camp Fire ignited on the morning of November 8, 2018, in Butte County, California, driven by sustained north winds of 35–65 mph and relative humidity as low as 7%. Within hours, it had destroyed the town of Paradise and generated a smoke plume visible from space. The Butte County Agricultural Commissioner's office reported that the fire burned through or directly adjacent to approximately 153,000 acres of crop and rangeland, while residual smoke affected over 2.3 million acres of California's Central Valley during the critical late-harvest period for tree nuts and field crops.
